The Arising COVID-19 Pandemic

Background for Early 2020

During February and March of 2020, the global community was in the early phase of the pandemic. The COVID-19 virus had already begun spreading internationally, firstly sourcing from East Asia. The World Health Organization had not yet declared COVID-19 a global pandemic until March 11, 2020. Many countries began implementing boarder restrictions and public health measures. In the South-East Asia regions, China had tens of thousands of cases, South Korea experienced a sudden surge, and Japan had localized outbreaks. The Western Pacific region had few confirmed imported cases comparably.

The Effectiveness of Proactive Measures

Context of Research

The Southeast Asia Region and the Western Pacific Region because countries in Asia and Oceania were among the first to respond proactively to COVID-19, shaped by both geographic proximity and their past experiences with outbreaks such as SARS (20022003) and MERS (2012). These regions implemented early border closures, strict quarantine systems, and large-scale public health measures that helped curb the spread of the virus during its first months. For instance, Singapore, South Korea, and Vietnam rapidly expanded testing and contact tracing, while Australia and New Zealand introduced border control policies earlier than many Western nations (Siahaan, 2020; Djalante et al., 2020). By examining the reported case numbers from February to March 2020, my research aims to highlight the effectiveness of these early interventions and compare the two regions.

COVID-19 Data of Early 2020

Data Used

The data set I used comes from the COVID-19 Data Repository by the Center for Systems Science and Engineering (CSSE) at Johns Hopkins University. It compiles daily case reports from national health ministries, the World Health Organization, and other verified sources, beginning on January 22, 2020. The data set is structured with rows representing countries/regions and columns representing cumulative case counts for each date.

In Figure 1., this is a stark difference in reported cases from each region. This gap between the regions reflects both epidemiological and policy differences. Some Western Pacific countries responded rapidly after SARS experience, for example, Vietnam closed borders with China in January, Singapore initiated aggressive airport screening. This difference can be seen in the reported data in Figure 2. In Most Read of 2020: Southeast Asias Responses to the COVID-19 Pandemic, stronger technical capacity in some states contrasted with delayed detection in others. The ASEAN case study Status Quo of outbreak and control of COVID-19 also confirms that lower early case counts were consistent with slower doubling rates, higher recovery ratios, and differences in active case trends across countries. While Western Pacific data reflects the epicenter of global spread, Southeast Asias low numbers illustrate how geography alone does not determine the spread, preparedness, policy, and detection capacity play a central role.
